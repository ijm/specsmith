## 1.Introduction – The Equilibrium Has Shifted

The existence of AI tools has shifted the core speed-rigor equilibrium, and our intuition for it needs updating. In this article we'll argue that the purpose of documentation has subtly changed, it is no longer just a tedious overhead, but a bottleneck in the primary way to communicate with modern tools.  Ignore this shift, and the same AI systems meant to accelerate us will slow us down.

Every project lives on a spectrum between speed and rigor. At one extreme are quick fixes, MVPs, and the “just ship it” mentality. At the other are detailed specs, formal processes, and documentation for every future possibility. For most projects this choice should lean toward speed, where the old Worse is Better, and first-to-market strategies were worth taking on technical debt.

That old equilibrium works because humans can fill in gaps. Institutional "common" knowledge, from tacit choices, experiences, and unspoken “well everyone knows this” shortcuts doesn't slow down a project. In fact, keeping thing informal is often seen as a benefit by keeping the project nimble. The stumbling and seeming cluelessness of project newcomers, until they absorb the culture, is precisely what made them newbies.

New AI tools break this system. Unlike humans, AI has no social intuition, no memory of hallway conversations, and no institutional common sense. It only knows what it was trained on and what’s written in its context. Which means many of the shortcuts we’ve always relied on like putting off documentation and letting knowledge live in people’s heads is no longer worth the vig.

In the following sections we'll explore what AIs need in tech documents, suggest some structure, and some immediately useful implications. Let's start with the historical balance.
