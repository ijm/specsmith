## 6. Conclusion – Why It Matters

The shift toward documentation isn't cosmetic. Tacit knowledge was always a fragile foundation, but as long as humans were the only interpreters, it worked well enough. Modern tools are increasingly introducing AIs as first-class actors. This makes the fragility of tacit knowledge visible. These systems can only act on what's written down. If the documentation is underspecified, ambiguous, or inconsistent, they will do exactly what the spec allows: generate slop at scale.

Recasting documentation as a compilable and testable specification isn't about process theater, forcing formality, or just ticking some box. It's about making the constraints of the system explicit so the end-to-end system, which now includes AIs and humans, behaves predictably. Documentation can no longer be an afterthought. It must be a structural component of the build pipeline—part of the project infrastructure, not commentary around it.

Why it matters is simple: the equilibrium has moved, and our intuition needs updating. Where once some rigor was optional and often deferred, now it defines whether these tools become force multipliers or slop multipliers. If you want leverage rather than friction from AI, the language and structure of your documentation have to meet LLMs where they are: closer to a compiler, not a mind reader.

Overall, our argument is simple: the more AI becomes part of the build pipeline, the more documentation must evolve toward a “compilable” artifact. That shift toward treating documents as executable specifications rather than commentary is what will determine whether AI amplifies our work or merely accelerates the production of slop.
